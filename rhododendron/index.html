About the Pacific Rhododendron

Alternate names

  • Rhododendron macrophyllum (scientific)
  • Rhododendron californicum (synonym to the scientific name)
  • Coast Rhododendron
  • California Rhododendron
  • California Rosebay

Habitat

The Pacific Rhododendron is the Washington state flower. It earns one if its other names, Coast Rhododendron, because of its proximity to the Washington and Oregon coasts, although it grows all the way from British Columbia to California, west of the Cascades. The plant prefers damp soil, although it can tolerate drier soil, and it will usually bloom mid-to-late spring, starting around late April.

Physical Features

This plant ranges from about three to about twenty feet tall, described as shrub to small tree in size. The flowers can be seen in many shades of pink, from pale pink to a deeper rose nearing violet. They are fluted in shape with a flatter top, and have five lobes instead of individual petals that spread out into a wavy star-like shape. It has 10 stamen of varying lengths, and only the longest protrude from the center. The plant has an interesting characteristic of producing thick and leathery evergreen leaves that protect themselves from colder and drier weather by growing the hairs on the underside and by rolling inward.

Cohabitants

The Pacific Rhododendron is found near evergreen trees like Western red cedar, Western hemlock, and Douglas fir, where it is considered an understory plant that thrives under the cover of the taller limbs of trees. Although both the leaves and flowers contain toxins that protect them from most wildlife foragers, deer and beaver may be seen eating the flowers and flowerbuds. Songbirds utilize the larger bushes for their nests, and the nectar attracts insects like bumblebees and swallowtail butterflies.
